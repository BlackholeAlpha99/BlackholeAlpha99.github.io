Introduction¶

Welcome to the data science pipeline tutorial. This tutorial is designed to walk newcomers through the data science process of data collection/curation and parsing, data management/representation, exploratory analysis, and hypothesis testing/machine learning, as well as effective communication of insights attained.

To do this, we will utilize the IMDb datasets publicly available on their website. These datasets contain information about the works documented and ranked on https://www.imdb.com/. We will use them to explore popular movies (defined in this tutorial as movies with over 100,000 ranking votes) and their evolution over time, with the purpose of demonstrating the importance of context when doing data science.

To resolve this, we need to merge the databases. This merge function takes multiple dataframes and combines them into a single dataframe. Missing Data¶

Sometimes, data goes missing. This could be from a variety of things: human error, errors in getting the data, bad formatting, etc. In any case, we must screen for any missing values. Fortunately, dataframes let us do this fairly easily if we go column by column. Most would first attempt something akin to below, attempting to use the isna function to check if something is NaN:

One complaint that I've heard a lot growing up from older generations is that they don't "make 'em like they used to." This can be in reference to a lot of things, but at least where I grew up was ultimately a product of a sense of "cultural degradation," which is a primarily white supremacist/colonialist mindset that "culture" (read: colonial/fascist ideals of assimilation and erasure of minority peoples and cultures) is being degraded by the other (non-white people, queer people, trans people, neurodivergent/disabled people, etc). (Note: There's an excellent video essay by Youtuber Jacob Geller deconstructing similar ideas in relation to modern art available at https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v5DqmTtCPiQ for those interested. Content warning for discussion of sensitive topics.)

Is this way of thinking a product of nostalgia combined with backlash at the social progress of the last century, or are movies actually getting worse?

Let's see if there are any trends among the distribution of movies across time by applying a trend line. At first glance, the distribution looks curved, so let's try a square fit.

Interesting. The square fit gives a line that's pretty much linear, which was not expected. It is also on a significant downward trend. Does this mean that movies are progressively getting worse, as many people would like you to believe? Let's see if we can account for some things.

First, let's iterate over time and count how many movies are in the system from that year or before, sort of like a cumulative distribution function except not normalized. Additionally, I am going to plot the year IMDb was created (1990) and the year it was moved to the web (1993), for reasons that will become clear later.

Conclusions¶

Now, there are very likely other factors playing into the structure of the data, such as potentially increasing markets for movies or higher volumes of movies being created. However, even with our admittedly limited perspective, there are very clearly two distinct periods of the database, with the inflection point of the graph coinciding with the creation of IMDb. Prior to the creation of IMDb, there are very few movies with over 100,000 ratings. However, after the creation and popularization of IMDb, the amount of movies with over 100,000 ratings explodes.

It is unfortunately unable to be tested in in this instance, as my personal computer does not have the hardware to run comprehensive analysis on a quarter of a million movies, but my personal theory regarding this is that movies that came out before the creation of the database have a substantially decreased amount of ratings and documentation compared to movies that came out after. It makes sense if you think about it; very few people are going to, upon hearing about the database, go through and rank every movie they've ever seen before the database was created. Rather, once the database was created, people would likely go to rank movies after they've seen them. This means that only the best movies from before the creation of the database - ones that people continually rewatch or think about - will get ranked, leading to the inflated average rating for older popular movies. Additionally on a pure quantity scale, the popularization of streaming services and increased internet and television access means that overall, it's also likely that more movies are being watched by more people. It's not that movies are getting worse, it's just that movies in general are getting critically analyized and categorized on a much larger scale, leading to the bad movies also getting a large amount of coverage and reviews.

This analysis is evidence that the most important aspect of data science is context. It is extremely easy to either manipulate data to fit one's viewpoint or make a false claim based on bad analysis. At all times, remember your confines. The set we worked with today was extremely narrow, due both to hardware limitations as well as the limitations of creating, maintaining, and updating a crowdsourced database off of popularity. We had to do a massive amount of filtering, as it's not exactly objective or ethical to say that an episode of a little-known tv show that a single person voted a 10.0 is better than The Shawshank Redemption with a score of 9.3 across 2.5 million votes. The limitations of your data and the methods by which that data was obtained are as important as the data they show, if not more so. If there's one tenet you come away with from this tutorial, it should be this.
